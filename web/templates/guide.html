Vowels

Pawnee has four basic vowel qualities. Each can be short or long (held about twice as long). Vowel length changes meaning, so pay attention to it.

About the middle vowel e

The Blue Book (Pâri Pakûru') uses e in some spellings. This sound sits between i and a — the tongue is relaxed but higher than for a. In the Parks dictionary, this is represented by the IPA symbols ə (schwa) or ɪ, and it's respelled as uh or ih depending on context.

Whispered Vowels

At the end of a word, after a hard consonant (p, t, k, s, ts), vowels are often whispered — barely voiced, more like a puff of breath. In fluent speech they may be almost inaudible. This is normal and not a mistake.

Accent Marks

Some forms in the dictionary carry accent marks (á, í, ú). These indicate high pitch on that syllable — Pawnee uses pitch rather than stress to mark emphasis. In the respelling, accents are not shown, but they can affect meaning.

Syllable Structure

In the dictionary's phonetic transcription, a dot (•) marks syllable boundaries, and a dash (–) marks stem boundaries. For example:

[•rə–hʊh–kaa–paa–kɪs•]
duh-hooh-kah-pah-kihs

Quick Reference: Reading the Dictionary

Each entry in this dictionary shows up to three forms:

  1. Headword — the Parks dictionary spelling (e.g., taraha)
  2. Respelling — an English-friendly pronunciation (e.g., tuhduhuh)
  3. IPA — the precise phonetic transcription in square brackets

The respelling is designed so you can read it aloud using English pronunciation habits and be close to the Pawnee sounds. When in doubt, the IPA form is the most precise guide.
