THE REBIRTH OF THE PAWNEE SCOUTS
In 1864, at the height of the American Civil War, Union Army
Major Frank North was authorized to raise and organize a company of
Pawnee Indian Scouts to be used as the `eyes and ears' of the Union
Army's Cavalry. The Pawnee Indian Nation was selected for mission
because Pawnee braves were regarded as among the most proficient
scouts and fierce warriors of any Indian Nation on the Great Plains.
Furthermore, the Pawnee Nation was also regarded by President
Abraham Lincoln as being among the most loyal to the United States,
during a conflict that divided the loyalties of not only the white settlers
and their families, but other Indian Nations as well. That loyalty on the
part of the Pawnees manifested itself repeatedly during the remainder of
that conflict which resulted in successive and decisive victories by the
Union Cavalry over Confederate forces whenever the Pawnee Scouts
were deployed.
The Pawnee Scout Company so distinguished itself that by the end
of the war it had been increased in size to that of a Scout Battalion and
transferred to the western frontier. The Union Pacific Railroad was then
under construction and the crews were being raided by bands of Sioux,
Cheyenne and Arapaho. The Pawnee Scouts were deployed in
reconnaissance—and sometimes in combat roles—against warriors of
those other nations. The Pawnee's skill, knowledge, courage and internal
discipline was so distinguished that in a decade that encompassed the last
two years of the Civil War and eight more years of frontier campaigning,
the Pawnee Scouts did not lose a single warrior in combat, yet they
accounted for considerable losses among enemy forces.
In 1874, after completing a decade of service in the armed forces of
the United States, the Pawnee Scout Battalion was honorably discharged
with distinction and the unit was deactivated.

FOREWORD
The publication of this book is a unique event in the history of
the Pawnee Tribe. The language which characterizes and conveys
all that is unique to our particular heritage is now in a form which
reveals its richness to a wide audience.
This event comes none too soon in the history of the Tribe. In
recent years more and more of the young members of the Tribe
have learned English as their mother tongue. The language which
distinguishes us from other tribes and peoples of the world is now
spoken fluently by only a limited number of our elders.
This book is valuable because only they could have written it.
To them we are grateful for the eager dedication they displayed
in the process of recording and analyzing the language.
The youth of the Tribe too, I am sure, will be forever grateful for
the precious legacy which has been handed down to them by their
parents and grandparents. We hope that the publication of this
book will signal a rebirth of interest in the Pawnee language and
that many of our youth will learn it well enough to pass on to their
children.
If this should not be the case, we are at least happy that we can
share some of our language and culture with the country at large.
Our libraries are filled with the written records of many cultures.
we are pleased that the Pawnee language will now take its place
among these distinguished documents.

Reading and Writing Pari Pakuru'
The Pawnee Language
The Pawnee language is not just a group of words or sounds or
letters; it is a way to talk to people and a way to listen to
people. No one can learn to speak Pawnee unless he listens to
and talks with people who speak Pawnee. This book is written to
encourage students to learn how to do this; it is a map or blue-
print for students and teachers of the Pawnee language who de-
sire to talk and listen to each other in this way.
There are other tribes such as the Arikaree of North Dakota,
the Wichita, and the Caddo who talk in almost the same way as
the Pawnee. We guess that at one time, all of these tribes were
one tribe, and that as various groups parted to go their own
way, they began to speak in their own way. However, Pawnee
and these other tribes have languages which are very similar to
each other in certain ways which make them tremendously dif-
ferent from other languages like English. Pawnees, like the
other related (Caddoan) tribes, talk to each other using very few
different sounds—where English speakers use about forty-eight
sounds, Pawnee speakers use only about seventeen (if you count
the long and short vowels).
The greatest difference between how Pawnee speakers talk
and English speakers talk, however, is the grouping of sounds
into "words." English speakers name things and events, whereas
Pawnee speakers describe things and events. For example, the
English "word" for a large woody plant is tree. A tree is a tree
whether it is standing or leaning or lying fallen on the ground.
However, a Pawnee speaker sees a tree standing there and says
ti ha .pi (there is standing wood). When he sees a tree lying fal-
len on the ground, he says ti hak•tsa (there's wood lying). As
you will learn, a Pawnee speaker actually makes up every word
from smaller pieces or roots which have meaning in themselves.
English speakers also do this, as in downgrade and upgrade, but

not nearly so much as Pawnee speakers. Of course, neither
speaker may realize what he is doing, but in learning Pawnee,

Interpretation Through Roots and Markers
In this book, what may be heard as long words will be written
as shorter, graphic words in order to help reveal the parts which
give meaning. The main parts of spoken Pawnee are called
roots. These normally stand for meanings like wood, go, and
red. The minor parts of spoken Pawnee are called markers and
are usually written together. They represent difficult meanings
like he or she did it, I am telling you what I saw, or it has al-
ready happened. Markers are hard to translate into English and
are really only understood after one hears and speaks Pawnee
for some time.
Roots and markers will be often shown clearly by interpreta-
tions or written pictures of what is the underlying structure of a
word or phrase. In an interpretation, roots and markers will be
joined by the plus mark (+).

Pronunciation Through Syllables
Even though the meaning of a word or phrase may be com-
posed of roots and markers, the pronunciation of a word or
phrase is composed of chunks of sounds called syllables. The
Pawnee speaker has an unconscious tendency to pronounce
words in pieces which begin with consonants and end with vow-
els. To show this tendency, we can write pronunciation using
parentheses and hyphens.
rak•tarih•karukus
Pronunciation (rak-ta-rih-ka-ru-kus)
Interpretation rak + tarih + ka + ruk + hus
wood + mark + places + make + -ing
pencil
Notice that pronunciation is written in parentheses, it divides
syllables with hyphens, and it marks long vowels with underlin-
ing.

Notes on the Alphabet
Consonants
1. Hard Consonants: p, t, k, ts, s
The stops are voiceless and without the breathiness or as-
piration of similar English sounds.
The complex consonant ts is often pronounced almost like
the English ch in church, especially whenever it begins a
xix

============================================================
PAGE 22
============================================================
syllable which is not the last syllable of a word.
2. Soft Consonants: r, (hr), h, w, '
The liquid r is a soft tap of the tongue against the roof of
the mouth: a very soft d with a sound near n or 1.
The combination hr is a breathy version of r which is pro-
nounced simply as h by Skidi speakers, so that the r is si-
lent.
Silent h: South Band speakers do not pronounce h at the
beginning of words; the h will be silent as in English honor.
Pawnee w is the same as the vowel u, and so wu sounds
like a long u. After h, w will take on a wh sound as in
English where.
The voice catch ' means a stopping of the voice in the
voice box, the kind of sound that breaks up the English ex-
pression uh-oh. At the end of words, it means the vowel is
cut short and a slight "echo" is produced.

Vowels
Since the letters used for Pawnee vowels differ from normal
English pronunciation, it will help the student to understand
how the vowels are actually made by the mouth, lips, and ton-
gue.
A vowel is a sound made with the mouth open and the air
coming through without stopping. Vowels are the sounds which
can be held or sung.
There are four main vowels in Pawnee, though the South
Band e is pronounced by Skidi speakers just as if `;, were an i.
Each of the Pawnee vowels may be long or shirt, but you can
recognize every vowel sound as one of the four vowels by paying
attention to the particular way it is made.
1. Round vowel u: the lips are pursed or rounded as in English
prune or push.
2. Close vowel i: the tongue is humped up and close to the roof
of the mouth; only a little air is allowed through the space
between tongue and roof of mouth.
3. Open vowel a: the mouth is open and relaxed, the tongue is
low in the mouth and relaxed. REMEMBER, even though a
often sounds like the u in English mud, use u only for the
round vowel. Think of the same sound written as a in above.
4. Middle vowel e: most of the time, e is used where i and a
could have joined together. When this vowel is made, the
tongue is in the middle of the mouth, somewhere between
the tight close vowel and the relaxed open vowel.
Whispered Vowels
You will notice that fluent Pawnee speakers will often drop or
whisper a vowel at the end of a word. That is, the vowel will be
pronounced, but very softly, without using the voice, only the
breath. This usually happens at the end of a word, after a hard

Accent
The accent mark " is used to indicate where the voice raises
to a higher pitch. Since this often occurs on a syllable with a
long vowel, it helps to predict where long vowels occur.
Accent is a feature of phrases and grammar. Each word when
said by itself has only one accent. Every phrase and simple sen-
tence has at least one primary accent. Just because you know
the accent of a word, however, does not mean that you necessar-
ily know whether or not that syllable will be accented in a
phrase or sentence.
ktiruks bear
(ku-ruks) (SB)
kuru kks•katit black bear
Unmarked Accent. If there is no accent mark on a word, it is
accented on the first syllable.
Primary and Secondary Accent. If in a phrase or sentence an
accent is marked, all other natural (unmarked) accents usually
flatten out.

PAWNEE KINSHIP
One of the cultural habits of the old-time Pawnees that still
survives today is the unique non-English way of using kinship
terms. There seem to be complicated rules for figuring out what
to call someone, but since the English system has been mixed in,
the important thing to consider is the difference between a "re-
spect" and a "joking" relationship, as suggested by Weltfish
(1965: Chapter 3).
A boy or man calls a woman atira and she calls him tiki or
son if she is responsible for his welfare and for giving him ad-
vice on how to act properly. This represents a relationship of
respect, and a man who calls a woman atira will call her sisters
and daughters atira also, as relationships of respect are inher-
ited. Atias is another term of respect. In the old days, the tribal
agent was called atias or father and other white persons were
likely to be called irari or brother, friend.
35

============================================================
PAGE 64
============================================================
Terms of a joking relationship are atipat or apat or grampa
and atika or gramma. A man may call a woman atika to repre-
sent a more informal relationship and a woman may call a man
apat or grampa to represent an informal, familiar relationship
which calls for teasing and joking.
Joking relationships are more easily created and two persons
may easily choose to call each other, for example, apat or
grampa and taka or sonny, whereas the use of atias, atira, and
other terms of respect are more strict and inherited.

The diet of the old-time Pawnee included buffalo and deer
meat, as well as corn, beans, and squash. After the hunt, or at

ture use. Most Pawnee food was boiled, and so soup and mush
were the staple diet.

pears before the object, if both are named by a noun; the English
order subject-verb-object is acceptable, but old Pawnee speakers
preferred both nouns together .

EPILOGUE
Our Way (Our People)
LIVING PAWNEE: Speaking, Singing, Writing.
The Pawnee language lives when people talk to each other in
their own way. Speech and conversation are the main ways but
not the only ways people talk to each other. Singing and writing
are important ways in which people talk with or communicate to
other people.
Listening to songs and reading any written material can help
you learn Pawnee. The special Pawnee language material in this
chapter can be used for classroom or private enjoyment of this
written, sung and recited material.

Translation of the Reading
WHERE WE USED TO LIVE
This our Pawnee tribe is made up of four bands; we are these:
Tsawii, Kitkahahki, Pitahawirata and Tskiri. And it (the
tribe) used to live, it is said, in mud lodges near the Platte
("Flat") River.
Twice a year they went hunting. It is said that they killed
buffalo and deer, (game). In addition, they planted various
things, corn, beans and squash. They would roast corn, and used
to make jerky and they dried the beans and squash. Perhaps
there were about thirty people dwelling in one mud lodge. Also
they would put their horses inside in the winter.
It is said that it (the tribe) had (many) customs which were
very wonderful. The old ones were apparently sincere (about
these things).
It must have been good land there where we lived. Those
other enemy tribes about (us), attacked us all the time. The
leaders, perhaps, decided to come down here to the south.
